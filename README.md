## Part I · Emmy Noether's 1918 Variational Symmetry Theorem

### I.1 Thought Experiment: The Lonely Particle and the Stationary Universe

Consider a single particle moving in three-dimensional space, far from any other matter. The particle has a Lagrangian $L(x, \dot{x}) = \frac{1}{2} m \dot{x}^2 - V(x)$, where $V(x)$ is its potential energy. We perform a thought experiment: shift the entire universe — the particle, its potential, its observers — uniformly to the right by an amount $\epsilon$. Does the particle behave any differently?

If the universe has no preferred origin — if space is homogeneous, with no special location — then the answer is no. The particle obeys the same equations of motion before and after the shift; the action functional $S = \int L \, dt$ takes the same value. The action is *invariant under spatial translation*.

Now consider the consequence. By the principle of stationary action, the particle's true trajectory is the one that makes the action stationary against arbitrary variations.
